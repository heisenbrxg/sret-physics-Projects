About Quantum Scattering & Double Barrier Tunneling

Quantum scattering describes how quantum particles interact with potential barriers. Unlike classical particles that simply bounce off or pass over barriers, quantum particles exhibit wave-like behavior leading to fascinating phenomena.

Key Concepts:

  • Quantum Tunneling: Particles can penetrate through barriers even when their energy (E) is less than the barrier height (V‚ÇÄ), defying classical physics predictions.
  • Reflection Coefficient (R): The probability that an incident particle will be reflected by the barrier system.
  • Transmission Coefficient (T): The probability that a particle will tunnel through and emerge on the other side.
  • Conservation Law: R + T = 1 (or 100%), ensuring probability is conserved.

Double Barrier Resonant Tunneling:

This simulation models a double barrier system - two potential barriers separated by a quantum well. This configuration produces remarkable effects:

  • Resonant Tunneling: At specific "resonant" energies, transmission probability approaches 100%, even though E < V‚ÇÄ! This occurs when the particle's wavelength matches the well width, creating constructive interference.
  • Quantum Well States: The region between barriers acts as a potential well that can trap particles temporarily, creating quasi-bound states.
  • Enhanced Transmission: When particle energy matches a quasi-bound state energy, transmission dramatically increases - this is resonance.

Real-World Applications:

  • Resonant Tunneling Diodes (RTDs): Electronic devices exploiting this effect for ultra-high-speed switching and oscillators.
  • Quantum Cascade Lasers: Use multiple quantum wells for generating terahertz radiation.
  • Single-Electron Transistors: Manipulate individual electrons using quantum tunneling effects.
  • Scanning Tunneling Microscopes (STM): Image surfaces at atomic resolution using tunneling current.
