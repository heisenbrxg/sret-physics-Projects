Explanation

This simulation demonstrates a simple version of Quantum Brownian Motion (QBM). The magenta-colored particle represents a microscopic particle trapped in an optical trap (a region of potential energy that keeps it near the center).

The trap applies a restoring force that pulls the particle toward the center. However, due to random quantum fluctuations (noise) and damping (energy loss to the environment), the particle moves in a jittery, unpredictable wayâ€”similar to how quantum particles behave in real systems.

- Trap Strength: Controls how tightly the particle is held near the center. Increasing this value keeps it more stable.
- Quantum Noise: Controls the randomness or fluctuation level. Higher noise makes the particle move more erratically.
- Damping: Represents friction or energy loss to the environment. Higher damping makes the particle slow down and stabilize faster.
- Reset: Brings the particle back to the center with no velocity.

In real quantum systems, such as electrons in metals or atoms in optical traps, their interaction with the environment leads to similar unpredictable behavior. This simulation visually captures that concept.
